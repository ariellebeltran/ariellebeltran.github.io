    • Introduction

    LiteSupport is a flashlight product targeted towards seniors with arthritis. While this was initially an individual project, it was later completed as a team project to further improve any imperfections. However my roles as the main 3D-modeller and designer of the flashlight didn't change.

    Initial Sketches

    I first started experimenting with the different ways I can manipulate the primitive shapes of circles and squares. From there I started to find a pattern of having the square inside the circle as a placeholder for the lightbulb while the circle itself are the handle bars.

    3D Model Iterations

    Rough 3D Prototypes

    To visualize the measurements needed in order to make the physical flashlight I made a rough model detailing how big the dimensions are for the handle bars and the lightbulb.

    The initial 3-D models were not only rough in appearance but also in measurements, this however helped me visualize how to properly measure the physical model with ease.



    Physical Model (Styrene)


    Medium to High Fidelity 3D Prototypes

    Upon comparing my initial 3-D model with the styrene model, I was able to take note of the physical constrains that I found from the physical model and implement it to the digital model. Here you can see a more refined curvature as well as a rounded back and front for the flashlight. The handle bars are smoothened out as well as a 3rd handle bar being added at the top to increase different interaction styles.



    Interaction Styles

    1. The user can use any of the 3 handles to pick up the flashlight, however the top middle flashlight (a.) is the most useful since it is not touching a surface when the flashlight is placed face up or down. Since the handles have a lot of space, their hands can easily slip through them to hold the flashlight. Their palms can face the inside of the handle to hold on to the inner part (b.) or outside to grip on the outer part (c.). When the flashlight is not being used, it does not lay flat on a surface as its faces are curved (e.) to leave a slight opening to allow the user to slip their hands through to pick it up.

    2. The user can hold the flashlight with one hand and adjust the level of brightness with the dial in front of them. Swiping right will increase the brightness while swiping left decreases. The user would either use their fingers (a.) or palm (b.) to adjust the level of brightness and feel soft clicks as they rotate the dial. It would reach the off-state once they swipe the dial all the way to the left (with no more possible rotations) while vice versa to right when they want the maximum brightness.Â 

    3. Depending on the size of the user s hand, it is possible to adjust the dial with just their thumbs if they want to hold the flashlight with two hands.

    4. When the user wants to put down the flashlight, whether it is turned off or on, they can do so by standing it up on the table. When it is turned on, the user can move the handles 90 degrees (a.) so that they are behind the base for improved standing support.


    Reflection

    Overall the final outcome of this project met the requirements needed for this product to suit a wide range of user needs for elders. The continued improvement and iterations made for each prototype shows the amount of considerations made for the user. If there were any improvements to be made for this particular flashlight it would be to make it more compact and portable.
